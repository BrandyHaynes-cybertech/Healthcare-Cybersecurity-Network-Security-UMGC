# Physical Security Plan — Northwest Shelbyville Regional Hospital
**Author:** Brandy Haynes, Security Analyst  
**Course:** CMIT 320 — Network Security | UMGC  
**Date:** November 2023  

---

## Overview

This physical security plan was developed for Northwest Shelbyville Regional Hospital (NWSRH), a 593-bed regional medical center. The plan covers security recommendations for the hospital exterior, lobby, and maternity unit, as well as staff security training requirements.

---

## Exterior Security

### Lighting
Lighting is a foundational security control. Poor lighting creates blind spots that enable bad actors to operate undetected. For hospitals specifically, enhanced lighting in parking lots and entrances:
- Improves surveillance video quality
- Deters harassment or harm to staff and patients
- Aids in identifying individuals during need-to-know or emergency situations

### Parking Lot Placement
Staff-only parking and entrances were proposed for several reasons:
- Enhances staff safety in the event of a bad actor
- Prevents patients from accessing restricted areas or interrupting medical staff
- Allows faster emergency response by placing staff parking closest to staff entrances
- Visitor parking is located near emergency room entrances to reduce congestion
- Enables easier enforcement of visiting hour curfews

### Camera Placement
Camera placement balances budget with effective coverage:
- All facility entrances and exits must have cameras
- Perimeter coverage uses multiple corner cameras plus a center camera per parking lot
- Visitor parking receives more cameras due to larger size and higher traffic
- All entrances and exits are covered regardless of access type

## Lobby Security

### Windows
Shatterproof or bulletproof glass windows are required per OSHA guidelines. In addition to protecting against weapons, this protects against environmental hazards such as hailstorms that could cause injuries or medical emergencies.

### Access Control

**For Non-Employees (visitors and patients):**
- Full name, address, and phone number (driver's license may be required in sensitive situations such as domestic violence cases)
- Time of entry and exit — supports headcount during lockdowns
- Visitors: name and location of the patient they are visiting
- Patients: appointment time, physician, and service location

**At Entry Points:**
- Security cameras at all entrances and exits
- Security guards at visitor entrances to maintain visitor logs
- RFID geofencing devices for real-time staff location tracking
- ID/badge scanners at all staff entrances
- Metal detectors to prevent weapons from entering the facility
- Visitor badges issued at entry and collected at exit

## Maternity Unit Security

### Nurses' Station Placement
The nurses' station is positioned at the front of the unit to:
- Allow rapid response to patient needs
- Provide easy access for patients and families to reach staff
- Enable nurses to monitor all incoming and outgoing unit traffic
- Deter infant abduction attempts

### Visitor Management
Visitors are directed to designated areas to:
- Prevent overcrowding in patient rooms
- Reduce spread of illness or infection
- Minimize unauthorized access to restricted areas
- Prevent infant abduction
- Help visitors navigate a large facility efficiently

### Newborn Security
- Matching identification bands are placed on both mother and newborn immediately after birth to prevent accidental infant switching
- Bands function as an alarm system — if a band approaches an exit, an alert triggers
- If a band gets too close to an exit, the system automatically locks down the unit and elevators to prevent potential abductions

### Secondary ID Badges for Maternity Staff
Secondary badges are recommended for maternity ward employees because:
- Redundancy is critical to patient and institutional safety
- The maternity ward is not a general-access area — secondary badge scanners at the ward entrance restrict unauthorized entry
- Emergency staff closer to the ward entrance can gain secure access without waiting for internal staff to open doors

## Security Training

### Training Requirements
All staff with access to hospital network data must receive:
- **Security awareness training** — identifying physical and social engineering threats
- **Cybersecurity training** — identifying data leaks, vulnerabilities, and breach indicators
- Training must be conducted periodically per **HIPAA Security Rules** and updated as the threat landscape evolves

### Training Levels (per IAHSS Standards)
Per the International Association for Healthcare Security and Safety (IAHSS), training is structured across three levels:

| Level | Target Audience |
|-------|----------------|
| **Basic** | General staff — introductory security knowledge and common daily challenges |
| **Advanced** | Staff with elevated system or data access |
| **Supervisor** | Security leadership and senior administrators |

Training depth is proportional to access level. A Senior Network Administrator requires significantly more advanced training than a lab technician or facilities staff member.
